Bayesian analysis of COVID-19 antibody tests with unknown specificity and sensitivity¶

This notebook implements a Bayesian framework first proposed by Gelman & Carpenter to infer the prevalence of COVID-19 based on a Santa Clara County antibody study by Bendavid et al. using PyMC3.

Bayesian model¶

Andrew Gelman and Bob Carpenter proposed a Bayesian inference procedure to estimate prevalence of COVID-19 from several sets of experiements conducted to evaluate the reliability of antibody tests. However, such estimation is hindered by the uncertainties around sensitivity and specificity, as the propogation pf which through a classical approach becomes extremly challenging. To circumvent that, one often builds hierarchical models to account for the variations across experiments. The following is a PyMC3 implementation of this procedure.

  • Hierarchical priors:

    • Let $\gamma_k$ be the specificity (TNR) at test site $k$ and $\theta_\gamma^{(k)} = \text{Logit}(\gamma_k) \equiv \log(\gamma_k)-\log (1-\gamma_k)$ be its log-odds. Assume a test specificity log-odds $\mu_\gamma$ and scale of variation $\sigma_\gamma$ such that the $\theta_\gamma$ is distributed as $\theta_\gamma^{(k)} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\gamma, \sigma_\gamma)$. In other words, the specificity $\gamma_k$ follows a logit-normal distribution: $$\gamma_k \sim \text{LogitNormal}(\mu_\gamma, \sigma_\gamma)$$ Note that we assume that there's no correlation between tests at different sites. Such choice will also pull $\theta_\gamma^{(k)}$ towards $\mu_\gamma$, with $\sigma_\gamma$ controls the inter-site variations in specificity. When $\sigma_\gamma \rightarrow 0$, one achieves a fully-pooled model where there's no variation across different sites. As $\sigma_\gamma\rightarrow \infty$, one has the no-pooling model where the specificity at each site can be estimated independently.

    • Similary, assume that sensitivity (TPR) at test site $k$ follows a logit-normal distribution $$\delta_k \sim \text{LogitNormal}(\mu_\delta, \sigma_\delta)$$

    • Prevalence: $\pi\sim U[0,1]$

  • Hyper-priors:

    • $\mu_\gamma, \mu_\delta \sim$ Logistic(0,1). This parameterization stems from the assumption that Logit$^{-1}(\mu_\gamma)$ (Logit$^{-1}(\mu_\delta)$), the probability of a incorrect (correct) test, follows a uniform distribution in $[0,1]$. More generally, one can show that if $X\in U[0,1]$, $$\mu + \beta \text{ Logit}(X) = \mu + \beta \log \left(\frac{X}{1-X}\right)\sim \text{Logistic }(\mu,\beta).$$

    • $\sigma_\gamma,\sigma_\delta \sim \mathcal{N}_+(0,1)$

  • Likelihoods:

    • Num. of positive observations: $n_{pos}\sim \text{Binomial}(N_{pos}, \delta)$, where $N_{pos}$ is the num. of subjects in the sensitivity test
    • Num. of negative observations: $n_{neg}\sim \text{Binomial}(N_{neg}, \gamma)$, where $N_{neg}$ is the num. of subjects in the specificity test
    • Num. of positive test results of subjects of unknown status: $$ n_{unk}\sim \text{Binomial}(N_{unk}, \pi\delta + (1-\pi)(1-\gamma)),$$ where $N_{unk}$ is the num. of subjects of unknown status

The posterior is therefore: $$P(\pi| n_{pos}, N_{pos}, n_{neg}, N_{nre}, n_{unk}, N_{unk}, ),$$ which characterizes what we know about the prevalence of the disease, given the observations of sensitity, specificity, and subjects of unknown status.

Note that we can alternatively use a parameterization of the binomials chance of success parameter on the log odds scale, where

$$n_{pos}\sim \text{Binomial}(N_{pos}, \text{Logit}^{-1}(\delta)), \qquad n_{neg}\sim \text{Binomial}(N_{neg}, \text{Logit}^{-1}(\gamma))$$

are used. Note also that $\text{Logit}^{-1}(x) = \text{logistic}(x) = 1 / (1+ e^{-x}).$

with pm.Model() as cov19_model:
    
    # Prior on prevalence
    pi = pm.Uniform('Prevalence', 0, 1)
    
    # Hyper-priors
    mu_g = pm.Logistic('mu_g', 0, 1)
    mu_d = pm.Logistic('mu_d', 0, 1)
    s_g = pm.HalfNormal('s_g', sigma=1)
    s_d = pm.HalfNormal('s_d', sigma=1)
    
    # Specificity, TNR
    gamma = pm.LogitNormal('Specificity', mu = mu_g, sigma = s_g)
    
    # Sensitivity, TPR
    delta = pm.LogitNormal('Sensitivity', mu = mu_d, sigma = s_d)
    
    # Likelihoods for sensitivity and specificity tests 
    # Note that one can alternatively use a parameterization 
    # of the binomials chance of success parameter on the log odds scale
    # To do so, replace delta by  pm.math.invlogit(delta) and similarly for gamma
    n_pos_lik = pm.Binomial('n_pos', sens_df['tests'], delta, observed = sens_df['pos_tests'])
    n_neg_lik = pm.Binomial('n_neg', spec_df['tests'], gamma, observed = spec_df['neg_tests'])
    
    # Prob of getting a positive result 
    p = pm.Deterministic('p', pi*delta + (1.0 -pi)*(1.0 -gamma))
   
    # Likelihood of an unknown subject
    n_unk_lik = pm.Binomial('n_unk', unk_df['tests'], p, observed = unk_df['pos_tests'])
